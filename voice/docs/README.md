This platform should be designed as a platform as a service that when it scales will support multiple users.

Speaker is the user, listener is sometimes the user, sometimes an external audience. So identity matters, but “sounding professional” matters more than perfectly mimicking their raw delivery. In practice that means you bias towards a clean Instructor style by default and let them opt into Story or News if they want a different feel.

Here is how I would architect this end to end, with the Whisper + Azure mix and pseudo-real-time in mind.

You start by treating everything in short chunks, roughly sentence sized. The user records or streams audio, but you buffer into 3–5 second segments. Each segment goes first into your self-hosted Whisper. Whisper gives you a transcript and timestamps. You use the timestamps for segmentation and for mapping back to the original waveform. You do not rely on Azure for text here, that is the cost saving and control win. From Whisper’s text and the user’s intended script (if you have it) you derive the reference text that Azure will use for scoring. For free-form speech you can take Whisper’s output as both “what they said” and “what we want to score”, for read-aloud exercises you keep the original script as the reference and use Whisper to sanity check it.

Once you have audio plus reference text, you call Azure’s Pronunciation Assessment endpoint, not the generic STT. You pass the audio file or stream, the reference text, and the assessment config that asks for word level and phoneme level scoring. Azure returns, for that chunk, which words are mispronounced, a score for each, optional stress and fluency metrics. This is where you get your “detect and assess” step. Because you already did ASR with Whisper, you can use cheaper Azure modes that focus on assessment and not on full blown transcription, which is where the cost saving comes from compared to “Azure only everything”.

On top of Azure’s output you build your correction logic. At minimum you will have a rule that says something like “if word score < threshold, mark for correction”. For read-aloud use cases, you simply snap back to the original script; for free speech you can decide if you want to keep their exact words or do light grammar cleanup. You now have a “clean target sentence” that you want spoken and a “dirty original recording” plus a map of where the errors were. This gives you the three artefacts you care about per chunk: original waveform, original transcript, corrected transcript.

The next stage is style selection and synthesis. You load or create a voice embedding for the user at session start from a small sample of their speech. That becomes the primary voice. You also have a small library of studio voices keyed as Instructor, Story, News. For each chunk you apply a prosody profile based on the content type. Since your speaker is the user but the listener is mixed, I would default to Instructor style for anything educational or explanatory and let them explicitly choose Story or News if they are doing narration or anchor-style work. You feed the corrected text, the chosen prosody profile and the user’s voice embedding into your TTS provider. If the provider returns quality metadata or you run a quick MOS-like heuristic (energy continuity, no clipping, normal duration), you decide if the synthesized chunk is “good enough”. If it passes, you use the cloned user voice. If it fails or produces obvious artifacts you regenerate in the same prosody style but with the studio narrator voice that best matches the context. For example, for a news article you use the News voice, for a chapter of a book you use Story.

Then you handle stitching. Because you are in pseudo-real-time, you can afford to be one chunk behind. That means the listener hears chunk n while you are processing chunk n+1. You never splice single words into the original waveform. Instead, for any chunk that had mispronunciations, you discard the user’s original audio and replace the entire sentence with your synthesized version. You then crossfade the tail of chunk n with the head of chunk n+1 in low-energy regions, normalise loudness and apply a light, consistent room or mic profile across the full session so that the noise floor and reverb feel uniform. This is where the “can they tell?” question is really answered. If your prosody profile is consistent and every “fixed” sentence has the same room signature as the unedited passages, most listeners will not notice you swapped the whole chunk.

Cost wise, the saving from self-hosting Whisper comes from the fact that you are no longer paying Azure to do ASR and assessment together for every minute. Whisper gives you unlimited transcripts for GPU cost only. Azure is used purely as a scoring engine. That usually lets you push your Azure usage to cheaper tiers or at least avoid paying twice for similar work. The TTS side will still dominate your bill if you regenerate a lot, but because you only regenerate chunks that were below threshold and you sometimes fall back to a shared studio voice, your per-minute effective cost tends to land closer to a couple of cents for analysis plus whatever fraction of a cent your TTS provider charges for the synthetic minutes you create.

From the user’s perspective the experience is simple. They select a project type, which implicitly sets the style profile (Story, Instructor, News). They speak. A second later they hear back a “clean” version that still sounds like them in a more polished mode, with the occasional line sounding slightly more like a pro narrator if the clone struggled. Technically, yes, someone with a trained ear might suspect that some lines were “touched up”, but they would not be sure, which is exactly the objective you described.
